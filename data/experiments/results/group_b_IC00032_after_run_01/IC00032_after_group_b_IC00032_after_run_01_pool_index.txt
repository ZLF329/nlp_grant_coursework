SPC is essential for maintaining periodontal health, intercept disease relapse, and minimize tooth loss and associated masticatory complications [2, 3]. As such, one way to address those barriers (which we will elaborate as part of this project) will be to apply the philosophy of skill mix, which allows a more efficient management of the dental practice resources. Currently, all NHS courses of treatment in primary care have a GDP responsible to start them through examination, diagnosis, and treatment planning. Clinical reality shows that GDPs do not have the possibility of sharing responsibilities with other dental team members (e.g. hygienist) due to the lack of workforce and time, therefore they often end up completing all SPC-related tasks [6, 7]. However, following contract prototypes embracing use of wider skill mix, the concept of flexible commissioning was introduced (2019), allowing local variations in primary care contracts to better meet patients’ needs. This project is in line with this new approach and NHS England can use it to validate flexible commissioning for SPC [7]. The use of a team approach to deliver the motivation and prevention phase, will free up time for the GDP to undertake more complex care, which will address the growing clinical time requirements for middle–aged patients [8, 9] who are more likely to require periodontal care. In the United Kingdom 70% of adults suffer from periodontitis, of which 10% present with severe forms [11]. It disproportionately affects the vulnerable segments of the population [12] and negatively impacts the quality of life [10], with a financial cost of $54 and $25 billion in direct and indirect treatment costs, respectively [10].\nPeriodontitis is an irreversible disease leading to tooth loss and may reappear shortly after treatment without adequate SPC (step 4 of therapy) [1]. According to current treatment guidelines [2, 3], SPC visits should last approximately 60 minutes and should include: medical history, re-evaluation (10-15 min); motivation, re-instruction and instrumentation (30-40 min); polishing, and determination of future recalls (8 min) [13]. No data are available on the adhesion/awareness to validated SPC within NHS or on the incidence of disease reoccurrence. This has resulted so far in a wide variation of SPC care delivered in terms of frequency, length and procedures.\nRecently, an NHS document [14] in conjunction with the British Society of Periodontology guidance on delivering phased care for periodontitis patients under Units of Dental Activity (UDA) banding clarified the possibility of providing phased treatments in periodontitis patients. In particular, it was recognized that time is required to undertake SPC and, as nsuch, a Band-2 course of treatment is permitted for engaging patients. During SPC dentists should repeat plaque scores, reinforce behaviour change, patient motivation, risk factor control, perform a detailed pocket charting, professional mechanical plaque removal and localised subgingival instrumentation of residual pockets. While this document is important as it recognisoes for the first time the importance of SPC in the NHS and provides some guidance on how to provide a staged treatment, it is not clear how it has been perceived and applied (if at all) by dentists, how much time they dedicate to SPC visits and how they decide on recall frequency.\nNo study has yet investigated the barriers to the correct implementation of SPC ins the NHS. We know that an important part of SPC is motivation to achieve good oral hygiene, but there is lack of evidence for this in primary care, so this could potentially represent a barrier and it is often attributed to time constraints or an NHS contract that does not support prevention [15]. A systematic review concluded that interventions targeting goal setting, planning of behaviour and monitoring by a health care professional/patient, are effective in promoting behaviour change [16]. A meta-analysis on improving health messaging in clinical practice also supported the use of a tailored face-to-face cognitivie psychological behavioural model to plan successful interventions in primary health care for increasing sustainmability of the behaviour [17]. Another potentially critical factor is the knowledge of the dentist. SPC visits under specialist care in higher socioeconomic groups lead to better periodontal stability than SPC delivered by a GDP [18, 19]. However, life-long SPC within NHS secondary care is not applicable nor affordable, as access to specialists is limited, ultimately leading to social inequalities. Remarkably, when GDPs are instructed with a clear prescription of SPC tasks, this difference tends to disappear [20]b, so it is plausible that the lack of proper knowledge/training might be another barrier.\nConversely, adequate visit length and an efficient distribution of tasks within the dental team based on the use of skill mix in the NHS (which includes hygienists and extended duties dental nurses) are likely to improve SPC management in primary care. Dental team working, where skill mix is widely used, has been shown to be beneficial for individual clinicians, teams, practice owners and patients [21]. Within primary dental care, there is both research and practice-based evidence about the clinical effectiveness, safety, and acceptability of using dental therapists and hygienists to provide preventative and diagnostic care to patients, alongside performing- a range of treatments to both adults and children [7]. Stage 1: Understanding implementation barriers and enablers\n5.3.1. Data collection\nThe research assistant will conduct semi-structured interviemws with key informant groups using a topic guide based on CFIR [22]. The updated CFIR was chosen because it breaks down contextual factors along several constructs organised in 5 domains relevant to healthcare interventions, namely innovation characteristics (8 constructs), outer setting (7), inner setting (11), implementation process (9), and patient individuals’ characteristics (13) [24]. Therefore, it provides a systematic approach to identify factors that can encourage or impede the bimplementation of SPC in primary care. Interviews will be conducted face-to-face in a quiet and private room in each GDP. Every interview, lasting 30-45 min, will be audio-recorded, professionally transcribed and de-identified.\n5.3.2. Data analysis\nInterviews will be analysed using deductive and inductive approaches. A deductive coding strategy will be first used wherein implementation barriers and enablers that are identified during the coding process will be organised according to CFIR domains. - In a subsequent stage, thematic content analysis via the framework method will be used to inductively develop categories from the raw data that reflecet factors affecting implementation to cover the possibility that they were not covered in CFIR. This inductive approach will permit systematic and visible stages to the data analysis process, moving through five stages (familiarisation, identification, indexing, charting, mapping and interpretation) [25]. Combining both approaches will help examine how CFIR r explains the findings and where it does not (thus allowing for theoretical contributions to the field), and can support in providing actionable, meaningful implications and recommendations locally.\nDouble-blind coding will be undertaken by the researcher assistant and a senior member of the research team (qualitative expert) independently at the start of the analysis process, who will then meet to refine the analytical framework, resolve disagreement and agree the final themes. The software NVivo (Lumivero, USA) will be used to manage data and to facilitate coding. Quality guidelines for the conduct of qualitative research will be adhered to [26].\n5.4. Stage 2: Co-design of the implementation strategy\n5.4.1. Identifying implementation strategies\nThe list of barriers and enablers identified in Stage 1 will be mapped to the implementation strategies that best address them using the CFIR-Expert Recommendations for Implementing Change (ERIC) matching tool [27, 28]. The ERIC is a compilation of 73 implementation strategies to mitigate or overcome anticipated barriers, which was developed by a panel of implementation science and clinical practice experts. The CFIR-ERIC matching tool links these two key resources to help users understand which ERIC implementation strategies will best address which CFIR barriers. The tool allows users to select the identified CFIR barriers; it then generates a prioritised list of high yield, targeted and relevant ERIC strategies that match each barrier. The list provides Level 1 endorsed ERIC strategies (>50% of experts ranked this as one of their top seven strategies for that barrier) and Level 2 endorsed ERIC strategies (20% to 50% of experts ranked this as one of their top seven strategies for that barrier). Users can thereafter select those strategies that best help to address each barrier and are most applicable to their context [27, 28]. To ensure consistency, two members of the research team will work together to map the selected strategies.\n5.4.2. Data collection\nA modified e-Delphi technique, including a combination of email invitations and two rounds of online surveys developed using Qualtrics®, will be used to reach consensus between the included participants on which implementation strategy to prioritise, as they might have different perceptions on barriers and their potential solutions. Using a consensus technique will ensure co-production of the implementation strategy, uniformity across included sites and teams, and engagement with later stages of the project. Participation will be anonymous to enable participants to comment freely and openly.\nThe list of recommended implementation strategies will be used to develop an online questionnaire, including relevant examples. This online questionnaire will be sent to all participating GDP practices, asking clinical teams and periodontal patients to indicate how important and feasible they believe each strategy is for SPC to be successfully implemented in primary care. Responses will be captured using 9-point ordinal scales (1 =least feasible/important, 9=most feasible/important). Reminders will be sent one and two weeks after the initial email. Participants’ responses will be collated and summarised quantitatively. In round 2, a revised list of items will be circulated for ranking after analysis and discussion by the research team. Consensus will be achieved when at least 51% of the panellists agree with the implementation strategy in terms of both importance and feasibility. To mitigate barriers to consensus, we will also report areas of residual non-consensus and describe the nature of the dissent.\n5.4.3. Data analysis\nn Descriptive quantitative analysis (response rate, median and interquartile range for each item) will allow ranking responses and identifying priorities. For each implementation strategy, we will calculate the median rating for both feasibility and importance. A total score will be calculated by adding together the median ratings of feasibility and importanceo for each implementation strategy. Then, strategies will be ranked based on their total score.\n6) Research Team\nThis is a multidisciplinary team with expertise in primary and secondary care in dentistry and public health.
